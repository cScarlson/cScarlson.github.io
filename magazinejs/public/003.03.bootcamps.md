## Breaking Code Over Multiple Lines
A lot of times I will come across some code that looks like this.

```html
<input 
    id="search" 
    class="form-control typeahead typeahead-small" 
    type="text" 
    placeholder="{placeholders.search}" 
    />
```

This is not _the worst_ if that is all it is. But we can see that it _does_ fit and read fine on just one line.

```html
<input id="search" class="form-control typeahead typeahead-small" type="text" placeholder="{placeholders.search}" />
```

It gets 10X more unwieldy when you have elements wrapping other indefinitely-nested elements, all of whom's attributes are broken over multiple lines for no sufficient reason.

Imagine if every book you bought used the same approach as example A. Your friends would think you are so smart because you could read a 1000-page book in the time it takes to read a 250-page book! That's not smart, that's pretentious. Those are roughly the numbers that I see in code all the time: I will condense a 1000-line file down to about only 250-lines. Remember: **you write code for _humans_, not _machines_**. Otherwise, our keyboards would have only two buttons, `1` and `0`.

### What is _really_ going on here
It is probably conspicious that the only time you really see this terrible practice is from those devs arriving in the workforce from _Code Bootcamps_. Seemed odd to me when I realized that. Then I spoke with a few bootcamp grads to realize: _this is actually how they learned it_.

If you are one of these developers writing code like example A -- STOP! Did you ever take a moment to ask _why_ your professor was writing their code this way? Your professor had to make a relatively tough decision when designing their curriculum: put their code and their notes on separate slides OR display them within the same slide. It's an obvious solution: you go with putting them on the same slide for obvious reasons. That means breaking it out in a way you normally never would.

I have seen insurmountable video tutorials do the same. What's most shocking to me is that these professors & tutorial authors never seem to actually mention it. I have seen _some_ state that "by the way, the reason my code is broken out all crazy like this is because I had to make room for my notes on the same slide" (Kevin Powel, for instance). In fact, the trade-off the profesor/author is actually making is likely due to an awareness of _Event Boundaries_. They likely understand that _if you have to change pages or scroll an entire page to look between both the notes and the code, you will likely not gather all the information without scrolling back & forth 20 different times_.


## "6 Figures In 6 Weeks!"
While I never went to a Code Bootcamp and didn't pay for a super fancy college, I feel the predatoriness of bootcamps has been egregious. I've worked with or known friends who did though.

Shortly after I arrived on the scene, there were a few industry players "bootcamping" people after teaching themselves. They only charges a small few thousand dollars but made high side revenue teaching others the way they, themselves, learned. Before long, the large colleges & universities caught wind and started competing at about the same pricetag. Maybe instead of spending $2,000-$4,000 you were now spending about $6K-$8K. Not terrible. About when I saw the first billboard stating "6 Figures In 6 Weeks!", the usual price for a bootcamp from a known university was anywhere from $16K to $20K. The big schools had muscled out the little guys and price-jacked your bootcamp by 10&times;, likely with lowered quality than you would have gotten from someone in the software industry looking for a 2nd stream of income.

Over the years, I've noticed that bootcamps only tought people that you know everything after about 6-weeks of training. It led to many disappointed people who could not get jobs and were deeper in the hole without enough to really get them out of it. Others pressed on but the "6 in 6" marketing scheme seemed to skew many incoming Entry Level Engineers', even Juniors', attitude to think they were way more experienced than they actually were. It led to plenty of whining as well because they were foced to reach for that arrow in their quiver when they had nothing else; they needed _any_ to make them more successful after their bootcamp left them in No Man's Land.
